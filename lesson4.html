Build Your First Budget.

You got your first paycheck. Let's make it work for you.

  • You worked all month. Your gross pay was $467
  • But when you check your account, it says $400

(Welcome to taxes. They took their cut before you even saw the money. That's a whole topic for later. For now, just know: $400 is what you actually have.)

In Lesson 3, you made trade-offs on a $50 weekend. Now we're doing it for a whole month. Same idea, bigger stakes.
  • $400 feels like a lot until it isn't
  • Phone bill, gas, food, hanging out... it adds up fast
  • That's why you need a plan before the money starts moving

That plan is called a budget. And it's simpler than you think.

Three buckets. That's it.

Forget complicated spreadsheets. You need three categories.

Gotta Pay
( needs )
The non-negotiables. Phone bill, gas or transportation, food when you're on your own. If you skip these, stuff breaks.
Good Stuff
( wants )
The things that make life worth living. Hanging out, clothes, streaming, entertainment. Wants, not needs, but that doesn't mean they don't matter.
Future Me
( savings )
Whether it's for something specific or just a cushion for when stuff goes wrong. Because stuff will go wrong.
  • A budget isn't a punishment
  • It's you deciding in advance where your money goes
  • Instead of figuring it out after it's already gone

You already know how to sort needs from wants (Lesson 2). You already know every dollar spent is a trade-off (Lesson 3). Now you're putting actual numbers on those decisions.

The 50/30/20 guideline.

A popular rule of thumb for budgeting. Here's what it says and why you shouldn't follow it blindly.

A lot of financial advice says split your money like this:

  • 50% Needs — half your income on the must-pays
  • 30% Wants — almost a third for the fun stuff
  • 20% Savings — a fifth toward the future
For your $400 paycheck, that'd be $200 on needs, $120 on wants, and $80 into savings.
  • That's a decent starting point
  • But your life might not fit neatly into those numbers
  • Maybe your needs are lower because you live at home
  • Maybe they're higher because you're helping your family with groceries

The split (ratio) matters less than the habit. Having any split at all beats winging it. You can adjust the percentages as your life changes.

The real lesson
  • A budget doesn't restrict your spending
  • It reveals your spending
  • It's the difference between "where did my money go?" and "I know exactly where it went because I told it where to go"
