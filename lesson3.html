The Trade-Off Game.

Every dollar you spend somewhere is a dollar you can't spend somewhere else.

Remember Jordan from Lesson 1? Blew $200 by Sunday.

  • Jordan didn't have a spending problem
  • He had a trade-off problem
  • He never stopped to look at what he was giving up every time he said yes to something
When you spend money on one thing, you're automatically saying no to something else. Every single time.

You've got $30. There's a hoodie you've been eyeing AND your friend's birthday dinner this weekend. You can't do both.

Whatever you pick, the other thing is what you lost.

That loss has a name.

This concept has a name.

When you choose one thing and give up another, the thing you gave up is called your opportunity cost.

Opportunity cost = what you give up when you make a choice. The road you didn't take. The thing you could've had instead.
  • You spent $15 on lunch? Your opportunity cost might be the $15 you could've put toward that concert ticket
  • Bought the new video game? Your opportunity cost is whatever else that $60 could've done for you

It doesn't mean you made the wrong call. It just means every choice has a cost you don't see on the receipt.

Quick check.

You have $25. You spend it on a T-shirt instead of saving it toward a $100 pair of shoes you want. What's your opportunity cost?

This is where needs vs. wants gets real.

In Lesson 2, sorting items was pretty chill. Now those categories are fighting each other for the same dollars.

  • Easy trade-off: New video game vs. saving for a car. One's clearly a want, one's working toward a bigger goal
  • Hard trade-off: Going out with friends vs. new shoes you actually need. Both feel important. Neither is "wrong"
  • Hardest trade-off: Two wants competing for the same money. Concert ticket OR new clothes. Weekend trip OR save for a laptop
Knowing your opportunity cost doesn't make the decision for you. It just makes sure you're making it with your eyes open.

So what do you do with this?

You can't avoid trade-offs. But you can make them on purpose.

The point of knowing your opportunity cost isn't to stress you out or make you feel guilty. It's so you stop being surprised when you can't afford something later.

Before you spend, ask: "What am I giving up by choosing this?" If you're cool with the answer, spend with confidence. If not, maybe hold off.
